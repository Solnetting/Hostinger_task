Critical reflection

Potential weaknesses

Every opinionated system has edges. These are the five bets the triage model makes — and what a next iteration could do about each one.

1
Weakness
T1 fatigue risk
If product teams ship T1 banners too liberally, users learn to dismiss without reading — eroding the tier's authority faster than any design decision can recover.
Possible solution
Enforce T1 via governance, not just design. Add a product-level gating rule (e.g. max 1 active T1 per user at a time, requires PM sign-off) and instrument dismiss rates per nudge type. A T1 with >60% same-session dismiss rate should be automatically demoted to T2 in the next release cycle.
2
Weakness
The 2-card T2 cap has no overflow path
Three simultaneous high-priority nudges have nowhere to go cleanly. The current answer — hide the third — is a bet that this edge case is rare enough to ignore.
Possible solution
Introduce a soft overflow state: a collapsed "1 more issue" chip below the two cards that expands inline on tap, without breaking the visual cap. This preserves the "max 2 visible" principle while preventing silent data loss. Alternatively, resolve the conflict upstream by defining a strict priority score so the third nudge is always demoted — not hidden.
3
Weakness
T3 is only as good as sidebar navigation
Contextual nudges in sub-sections rely on users visiting pages they may never open. Passive users with a Domains issue will miss the sidebar dot entirely.
Possible solution
Add a lightweight "You have issues in 2 sections" ambient indicator on Home — not a full nudge card, just a subtle line below the quick-actions grid. This bridges passive users to T3 surfaces without promoting contextual nudges to T2 priority. Track click-through from the indicator vs. organic sidebar navigation to validate whether it's worth the visual cost.
4
Weakness
No recovery path after dismissing a T2 card
Dismissing without acting routes users to the sub-section — but only if they remember. There is no undo, no history surface, and no second chance.
Possible solution
A 5-second undo toast on dismiss is the lowest-effort fix and covers the accidental-dismiss case without adding a full notification center. For intentional dismissals, consider a "Remind me in 3 days" snooze option surfaced inside the dismiss flow — this converts a lost nudge into a scheduled re-engagement rather than a dead end.
5
Weakness
Untested under real user load
The system was designed desk-based. Hostinger users managing 10+ domains and billing issues simultaneously may surface interaction patterns this triage model doesn't account for.
Possible solution
Run a moderated usability test with 5 power users (3+ active domains, recent billing event). Key questions: does the tier hierarchy map to their mental model of urgency? Do they notice T3 dots organically? Is the T4 drawer pull-only pattern discoverable? Complement with a 2-week instrumentation sprint — dismiss rate, time-to-action, and T3 section visit rate per nudge type — before locking any tier rules in production.
